Leading Causes of Death in the United States

This project analyzes National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) data to explore trends and patterns in U.S. mortality across time, states, and age-adjusted death rates.

Note: Some visualizations are static images (PNG) and others are interactive Plotly charts embedded as HTML.

Overview of the Analysis

This project presents an interactive data visualization analysis of the leading causes of death in the United States using mortality data from the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS). The visualizations examine how death rates change over time, how outcomes differ across states, and why age-adjusted death rates are essential for making fair comparisons between populations.

Rather than focusing only on which causes account for the most deaths, the analysis emphasizes underlying patterns such as long-term trends, geographic disparities, year-to-year variability, and the distinction between total deaths, crude rates, and age-adjusted death rates. Viewing mortality through multiple perspectives helps reveal insights that national averages alone can obscure.

This website is designed for public health students and policymakers and public health officials. Students can use the visualizations to practice interpreting epidemiological data and developing research questions, while decision-makers can use them to identify where mortality burdens are concentrated, where progress has slowed, and where targeted interventions may have the greatest impact.

The Overview section summarizes major patterns across causes, the Geographic & State-Level Disparities section highlights differences between states, and the Heart Disease Insights section provides a deeper examination of one of the most significant causes of death in the United States.

Visualization 1: Heart Disease Over Time

    This line chart shows the age-adjusted death rate for heart disease in the United States across multiple decades. The visualization reveals a strong and sustained decline from the late 1990s through the early 2010s, likely reflecting improvements in medical treatment, early detection, and public health interventions. In more recent years, the rate of decline slows and slightly reverses, suggesting that earlier gains may be plateauing. This pattern may indicate emerging challenges such as aging populations, lifestyle-related risk factors, or disparities in access to healthcare that are limiting further reductions in heart disease mortality.

Visualization 2: Treemap

    The treemap displays the relative contribution of each major cause of death to overall mortality in the United States. It clearly shows that a small number of causes, most notably heart disease and cancer, account for a disproportionately large share of total deaths. This concentration highlights how overall mortality trends are heavily influenced by just a few dominant causes. The visualization also emphasizes that many other causes, while important, contribute relatively small portions individually, suggesting that targeted efforts focused on the leading causes could yield the greatest overall impact on national mortality.

Visualization 3: Scatter Matrix

    The scatter matrix illustrates relationships between total deaths, crude death rates, and age-adjusted death rates. While total deaths and crude rates tend to increase together, the visualization shows that age-adjusted rates often diverge, reflecting differences in population age structures. This highlights that high death counts do not always correspond to higher underlying mortality risk. The visualization reinforces the importance of age adjustment when comparing mortality across states or causes, as it allows for fairer comparisons that are not distorted by demographic differences.

Visualization 4: Choropleth

    This choropleth map visualizes how death rates for a selected cause vary across U.S. states. Clear geographic patterns emerge, with some regions consistently exhibiting higher mortality than others. These spatial differences suggest that mortality is influenced by more than just national-level factors and may be shaped by regional conditions such as healthcare access, economic inequality, environmental exposure, and cultural behaviors. The map demonstrates how location plays a critical role in shaping health outcomes and highlights the importance of state-level policy and intervention strategies.

Visualization 5: Heatmap

    The density heatmap shows how mortality levels for different causes fluctuate over time and whether multiple causes peak during the same periods. The visualization reveals that certain causes experience overlapping increases in mortality during specific years, suggesting the presence of shared external influences. These could include nationwide public health crises, environmental events, or systemic stressors affecting multiple health outcomes simultaneously. By highlighting synchronized patterns, the heatmap encourages deeper investigation into broader forces that may impact mortality across several causes at once.

Visualization 6: Box Plot

    This box plot compares the year-to-year variability in total deaths across different causes. Some causes show narrow distributions, indicating relatively stable mortality trends over time, while others exhibit wider spreads and prominent outliers. These differences suggest that certain causes are more sensitive to short-term fluctuations or external shocks, such as epidemics or behavioral changes. The visualization helps distinguish between causes with predictable long-term patterns and those that may require closer monitoring due to volatility.

Visualization 7: Bubble Chart

    The bubble chart combines multiple mortality metrics by plotting death rates while using bubble size to represent total deaths. The visualization demonstrates that causes or states with the highest number of deaths do not necessarily have the highest mortality rates once population size is accounted for. This contrast highlights how raw death counts can be misleading without contextual rate-based measures. By integrating magnitude and rate information, the chart provides a more nuanced understanding of mortality risk and emphasizes the importance of multidimensional analysis.

Visualization 8: Age Impact Comparison

    This bar chart compares age-adjusted death rates for causes that primarily affect older populations versus those impacting all age groups. The visualization suggests that causes associated with older populations tend to exhibit higher age-adjusted death rates, reflecting increased vulnerability with age. The inclusion of error bars highlights variability over time, reinforcing that these differences are not static. This comparison underscores the critical role of demographic structure in shaping mortality patterns and the importance of considering age when designing public health interventions.

Visualization 9: State-Level Line Map of Heart Disease Trends

    The line map illustrates how death rates for a specific cause evolve over time across different states. While many states follow similar overall trends, the visualization reveals meaningful differences in the timing and magnitude of changes. Some states show faster improvements, while others lag behind or experience slower declines. This suggests that national averages can mask important state-level dynamics and that localized policies, healthcare systems, and social conditions play a significant role in shaping mortality outcomes.

Visualization 10: Violin

    The violin plot displays the full distribution of death rates across states for a given cause, capturing both central tendencies and variability. The shape of the distribution reveals whether most states cluster around similar mortality levels or if substantial disparities exist. Long tails and asymmetries highlight outlier states with unusually high or low death rates compared to the national pattern. This visualization helps identify uneven health burdens across the country and points to areas that may require targeted investigation or intervention.

Geographic & State-Level Disparities

Mortality rates vary widely across U.S. states, revealing geographic disparities driven by differences in healthcare access, socioeconomic conditions, and local policies.

Visualization 4: Heart Disease Age-Adjusted Death Rate by State (2017)

Deeper Insight

This choropleth map reveals pronounced geographic disparities in heart disease mortality across the United States in 2017. Rather than a uniform national pattern, heart disease death rates cluster regionally, with several states—particularly in parts of the Southeast and Midwest—exhibiting substantially higher age-adjusted rates than states in the West and Northeast.

Because the data are age-adjusted, these differences cannot be explained simply by older populations. Instead, the map points to deeper structural and social factors shaping cardiovascular health outcomes, including differences in socioeconomic status, access to preventive care, prevalence of chronic conditions such as obesity and diabetes, dietary patterns, physical activity environments, and the strength of state-level public health systems. The visualization demonstrates that where someone lives plays a critical role in their risk of dying from heart disease.

Who benefits & what they can do

  • Public health students: This map provides a clear visual foundation for studying health disparities and regional inequality. Students can use it to explore how social determinants of health, healthcare access, and policy environments contribute to uneven outcomes, and to compare high- and low-performing states for deeper causal analysis.
  • Policymakers & public health officials: By identifying states with persistently high heart disease mortality, this visualization supports targeted intervention rather than national one-size-fits-all solutions. Officials can use the map to prioritize funding, tailor prevention programs, and evaluate whether existing state-level policies are effectively reducing cardiovascular risk.

Visualization 6: Box Plot — Annual Variability in Death Rates by Cause

Deeper Insight

This box plot highlights how stable or volatile mortality rates are for major causes of death over time. Causes such as heart disease and cancer have relatively narrow interquartile ranges, indicating that although their death rates are high, they tend to change gradually from year to year. This reflects the chronic nature of these conditions and the slow-moving influence of medical advances and population-level risk factors.

In contrast, causes like stroke and unintentional injuries display greater variability, with wider spreads and more extreme values. This suggests that mortality for these causes is more sensitive to short-term factors such as policy changes, environmental conditions, economic shifts, or sudden public health shocks. The log scale further emphasizes that variability matters not just in absolute terms, but proportionally across causes.

Who benefits & what they can do

  • Public health students: This visualization helps students distinguish between causes driven by long-term structural trends and those that are more responsive to short-term interventions. It encourages thinking about predictability, volatility, and how different health outcomes respond differently to policy and environmental changes.
  • Policymakers & public health officials: Causes with higher variability require flexible and responsive policy planning, as sudden changes can quickly increase mortality. More stable causes, while less volatile, demand sustained long-term investment. This chart supports strategic planning by showing where rapid-response capacity versus long-term prevention is most critical.

Visualization 9: State-Level Heart Disease Death Rate Trends Over Time (Line Map)

Deeper Insight

While national averages suggest a single shared story, this state-level visualization reveals a far more complex reality. Although most states experienced declining heart disease mortality, the speed, timing, and consistency of those declines vary substantially. Some states show early and sustained improvements, while others decline more slowly or flatten earlier.

These differences highlight the importance of local context. Variations in healthcare infrastructure, insurance coverage, preventive screening, socioeconomic conditions, diet, physical activity environments, and state-level public health policies all contribute to divergent outcomes. The visualization demonstrates that heart disease is not just a national issue but a geographically uneven one, shaped by where people live as much as by individual risk factors.

Who benefits & what they can do

  • Public health students: This map encourages students to move beyond national narratives and think spatially about health outcomes. It supports comparative analysis between states and invites investigation into how policy, funding, and social conditions translate into measurable mortality differences.
  • Policymakers & public health officials: By identifying states that lag behind or plateau earlier, this visualization helps target state-specific interventions. Officials can benchmark their state against peers, evaluate which policies are associated with faster improvements, and justify reallocating resources toward regions where heart disease remains persistently high.

Visualization 10: Violin Plot — State Distribution of Death Rates by Cause (2017)

Deeper Insight

The violin plot reveals how death rates are distributed across states for each cause, moving beyond averages to show the full range of outcomes. For heart disease and cancer, the wide shapes indicate substantial state-to-state variation, meaning that while national averages appear stable, individual states experience very different mortality burdens.

Long tails and asymmetry in several violins highlight the presence of outlier states with unusually high or low death rates. This suggests that national statistics can obscure localized crises or successes. The visualization underscores that mortality risk is unevenly distributed and heavily influenced by state-level conditions such as healthcare access, socioeconomic inequality, and public health infrastructure.

Who benefits & what they can do

  • Public health students: This chart helps students understand distributional thinking—why means alone are insufficient and how disparities manifest across geographic units. It encourages analysis of why certain states fall into the tails of the distribution.
  • Policymakers & public health officials: The violin plot supports benchmarking and targeted action. States in the upper tails may require urgent intervention, while lower-performing states can serve as models for effective policies and prevention strategies.

Heart Disease: Focused Insights

A deeper, policy-relevant analysis of heart disease mortality across time and U.S. states.

Visualization 1: Heart Disease Age-Adjusted Death Rate Over Time (United States)

Deeper Insight

This visualization highlights one of the most significant public health successes in recent U.S. history: a sustained decline in heart disease mortality from the late 1990s through the early 2010s. The steady downward trend reflects the combined impact of advances in cardiovascular medicine, widespread risk-factor awareness, and large-scale public health interventions targeting smoking, cholesterol, and blood pressure.

However, the slowdown and slight reversal in more recent years is particularly important. Rather than continued improvement, the trend begins to plateau, signaling that earlier strategies may be reaching diminishing returns. Emerging challenges such as rising obesity and diabetes rates, sedentary lifestyles, population aging, and unequal access to preventive care are increasingly offsetting past gains. This marks a transition from rapid improvement to a period where progress is more difficult and uneven.

Who benefits & what they can do

  • Public health students: This chart provides a clear example of how population-level health outcomes respond to policy, medical innovation, and behavioral change over time. Students can use it to frame research questions about why declines slowed, compare heart disease to other causes of death, and explore the limits of traditional prevention strategies.
  • Policymakers & public health officials: The plateau serves as a warning signal, suggesting that maintaining progress will require new approaches—such as addressing social determinants of health, expanding preventive care in underserved populations, and shifting from treatment-focused strategies to long-term lifestyle and environmental interventions.
